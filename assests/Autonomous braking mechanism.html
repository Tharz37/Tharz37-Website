A-Baja SAE: Engineering a Brake

The Challenge: A Compact, Dual-Action Brake

During my internship with the A-Baja SAE team [Team Equinox], my teammate Rishikesh R and I were given a critical task: design a mechanism for both autonomous and manual braking. The requirements were intense.

The system had to generate 1000N of force, achieve a 10x mechanical advantage, and fit within an extremely tight 60x30x15cm space constraint. This wasn't a simple parts-fitting job; this was a ground-up design and engineering problem.

The Gauntlet: A Tale of Four Failed Mechanisms

Our journey was a rapid-fire brainstorming and testing process. We analyzed four classic mechanisms, but each one hit a wall.

1. Four-Bar Linkage: Our first idea. The problem? To get 1000N of force, the simulation required a 1-meter-long rod. This was impossible to fit in our tiny space.
2. Slider-Crank: This was more compact, but the mechanism introduced significant unwanted vibrations, which would have been disastrous for the buggy.
3. Bell Crank: This looked promising and fit the space, but our simulation results were clear: the mechanism simply could not withstand the immense torque and would fail.
4. Scotch Yoke: This was a solid option for motion, but it demanded a high-torque motor that we did not have and couldn't fit into the budget or power constraints.

Victory: A Return to Fundamentals

After exhausting these complex mechanisms, we realized the answer wasn't more complexity. It was simplicity. We went back to basics and designed a custom pulley system.

It was the perfect solution: it was incredibly compact, had no vibrations, and easily provided the 10x mechanical advantage we needed to generate 1000N of force. This design was successful, and our team went on to achieve All India Rank 7 and Kerala Rank 2nd. This internship was a powerful lesson in simulation, persistence, and knowing when a simple, elegant solution is superior to a complex one.
